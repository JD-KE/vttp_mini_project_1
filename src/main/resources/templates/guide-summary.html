Summary

Below is a summarized version of the guide to serve as a basic introduction of why and how to purchase a solar PV system in Singapore. To learn more about individual sections, please click on the relevant sections in the sidebar.

Benefits

  • Save on electricity costs by using solar generated electricity instead of puchasing it and/or selling back excess generated electricity to the electrical grid.
  • Pays for itself within its lifetime, even as soon as 5-7 years under optimal conditions.
  • Additional revenue from selling RECs based on the PV system's electrical generation
  • A solar PV system typically raises property value of the building in which it is installed.
  • Additional roof protection from direct exposure
  • Use clean energy instead of energy mostly generated by burning natural gas, coal, and other petroleum products
  • Can be integrated with other equipment to provide monitoring of overall house electrical usage and solar generation, energy storage for backup power/grid independence, etc.

Who are suited to get solar PV systems?

  • Building/Land Owners
  • Owners of units with ownership of the roof in a property (HDB/Condo Units)

Most solar PV systems in Singapore are installed on buildings, typically on the roofs. If you own a landed property with available roof space, you can consider getting a solar PV system for the building.

  • Alternatively, for those who own land with available space, ground based solar can be a viable choice depending on the conditions. Other possible variants can be Building Integrated PV systems to make full use of the empty areas on the building, PV systems on other fixed infrastructures like walkways, or floating PV for owned water spaces.

For those who own individual units in owned property e.g. HDB flats/Condominium units, getting a typical roof-based PV system out of the question except for owners who actually own the roof of the property (comes with additional caveats). It is possible to get small solar panel kits not connected to the grid to generate electricity for small scale use but users may not get the full benefits of a larger roof-based system.

What to prepare for contractor?

Below is a non-exhaustive list of information to prepare for the contractor to design and install the solar PV system.

  • New building (Installation only happens after receiving Certificate of Statutory Completion (CSC) or Temporary Occupation Permit), or existing building
  • Building Plans (Most important is the roof plan, floor plans also needed for components other than solar panels)
  • SLD for the building for electrical design and connection
  • Electricity Retailer electricity is purchased from; to determine payment method for sellback of solar generated electricity
  • Monthly electricity bill and when electricity is used; for estimation of savings, and additional modification of system
  • Request for certain equipment usually paired with solar PV system if contractor can offer and is suitable for owner (E.g. Energy Storage System, Electric Vehicle Charger).

The contractors will also guide their clients through the process. Make sure to ask what the information/details they ask from you are for.

Types of payment

Buying a solar PV system can be a large purchase, especially if there is a large roof area on which to install. Thus, solar PV contractors provide multiple ways to pay for the system.

  1. Lump sum payment
  2. Rental/lease of PV system
  3. Mix of the above

There are three types of payment which are typically used in Singapore, with the first two being the most common.

Lump sum payment is basically paying for the PV system in full by the end of installation. Typically, unlike what it implies, payments are done in stages, corresponding to the stage of progress of the installation. Some owners pay out of pocket, or take a loan (some banks do offer special offers for solar PV installation in Singapore).

Rental/lease of PV system can be done in a few ways. It can be in the form of fixed monthly payments, or payments based on the amount of electricity used/generated (more commonly known as a Power Purchasing Agreement (PPA)).

Government Agencies Involved

  • SP Group (by extension EMA)
  • SCDF

Typically, the contractor will handle these interactions with the authorities over regulations, but some forms require the owners to authorise certain things, such as approving net export rebate (sellback of excess solar generated electricity). In terms of government agencies, SP Group regulates and inspects the installation of solar PV systems (electrical) in buildings connected to the electrical grid, as well as the transmission of the generated electricity into the grid as well as electrical safety matters; while SCDF does the same but with regards to fire safety of the PV systems

There may be other government/regulatory agencies involved, typically depending on the location of the building. E.g. URA for buildings in certain development zones.
